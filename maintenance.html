DIY Repair Guides

Step-by-step guides for the most common bike repairs

Fix a Flat Tire

Easy

The #1 most common repair. Learn to patch or replace an inner tube in under 10 minutes.

  • Remove the wheel from the bike
  • Unseat the tire with tire levers
  • Remove and inspect the inner tube
  • Patch the hole or install new tube
  • Reseat tire and inflate to proper PSI

10-15 min | Tools: tire levers, pump, patch kit

Detailed Steps:
  1. Flip the bike upside down or use a stand. Release the brake if needed and loosen the quick-release or axle nuts.
  2. Deflate the tube fully. Insert a tire lever under the bead about 2 inches from the valve. Hook it to a spoke.
  3. Slide a second lever along the rim to unseat one side of the tire completely.
  4. Pull out the inner tube. Inflate it and listen/feel for the leak. Mark it with a pen.
  5. Rough up the area around the hole with sandpaper. Apply vulcanizing glue, wait 30 seconds, press patch firmly.
  6. Run your fingers inside the tire to feel for thorns, glass, or wire that caused the flat.
  7. Slightly inflate the new/patched tube so it holds shape. Tuck it into the tire starting at the valve.
  8. Push the tire bead back onto the rim using your hands (avoid levers here to prevent pinching the tube).
  9. Inflate to the PSI listed on the tire sidewall. Spin the wheel to check it's seated evenly.
Pro Tip: Always carry a spare tube on rides. Patching is great for the garage, but a fresh tube is faster on the road.

Adjust Your Brakes

Easy

Spongy or squeaky brakes? Dial them in for reliable stopping power.

  • Identify brake type (rim, disc, or coaster)
  • Check pad alignment and wear
  • Adjust cable tension with barrel adjuster
  • Center the brake calipers
  • Test squeeze and stopping distance

5-10 min | Tools: hex wrenches, cable puller

For Rim Brakes (V-Brakes):
  1. Check that brake pads are hitting the rim flat, not the tire. Loosen the pad bolt to reposition if needed.
  2. Pads should be 1-2mm from the rim on each side. Use the barrel adjuster on the lever to fine-tune.
  3. If one pad is closer than the other, use the small screw on the brake arm to center them.
  4. Replace pads if the grooves are worn smooth or if you can see the metal backing.
For Disc Brakes:
  1. Loosen the caliper mounting bolts slightly so the caliper can move.
  2. Squeeze the brake lever firmly to center the caliper on the rotor, then tighten bolts while holding lever.
  3. Spin the wheel. If you hear rubbing, fine-tune by loosening one bolt slightly and nudging the caliper.
Pro Tip: Never touch disc brake rotors with your fingers. Oil from skin causes contamination and squealing.

Clean & Lube Your Chain

Easy

A clean chain shifts smoother, lasts longer, and saves you money on drivetrain parts.

  • Apply degreaser to the chain
  • Scrub with a chain cleaning tool or brush
  • Rinse and dry thoroughly
  • Apply chain lube (wet or dry type)
  • Wipe excess lube with a rag

10-15 min | Tools: degreaser, brush, chain lube, rag

Detailed Steps:
  1. Shift to the smallest chainring and smallest cog so the chain has maximum slack.
  2. Apply degreaser generously along the chain while slowly back-pedaling. Let it sit 2-3 minutes.
  3. Use a stiff brush or chain cleaning device to scrub each link. Pay attention to the rollers.
  4. Rinse with water (avoid high-pressure directly at bearings). Dry with a clean rag.
  5. Apply one drop of lube per chain link on the inside (roller side). Back-pedal 20-30 revolutions.
  6. Wait 5 minutes, then wipe off all excess lube with a clean rag. The lube should be IN the rollers, not on the outside.
Pro Tip: Use DRY lube in summer/dry conditions and WET lube in winter/rain. Dry lube attracts less dirt.

True a Wheel

Medium

Fix a wobbly wheel by adjusting spoke tension. A critical skill for any serious rider.

  • Mount the wheel and spin to find the wobble
  • Mark the high spot with tape
  • Identify which spokes to tighten/loosen
  • Make quarter-turn adjustments with spoke wrench
  • Recheck and fine-tune until straight

15-30 min | Tools: spoke wrench, truing stand (or zip ties)

Detailed Steps:
  1. If you don't have a truing stand, flip the bike and use the brake pads as reference points.
  2. Spin the wheel slowly and note where it pulls left or right relative to the brake pads.
  3. Spokes on the RIGHT side pull the rim right when tightened. Left side pulls left.
  4. At the wobble point: if the rim goes RIGHT, tighten the LEFT spoke(s) and loosen the RIGHT spoke(s) by 1/4 turn.
  5. Work in small increments (1/4 turn max). Over-tightening can crack the rim or break spokes.
  6. Recheck after each adjustment. Repeat until the wobble is under 1mm.
Pro Tip: Always adjust spokes in pairs (one tighter, one looser) to maintain even tension across the wheel.

Adjust Seat Height & Position

Easy

Proper saddle height prevents knee pain and maximizes pedaling power.

  • Stand next to bike, saddle should be at hip height
  • Sit on saddle, put heel on pedal at bottom
  • Leg should be fully extended (not locked)
  • Adjust fore/aft so knee is over pedal spindle
  • Tighten seat clamp to proper torque

5 min | Tools: hex wrench (4mm or 5mm)

The Heel Method:
  1. Loosen the seat post clamp bolt with a 4mm or 5mm hex wrench.
  2. Sit on the saddle and place your HEEL on the pedal at its lowest point (6 o'clock).
  3. Your leg should be completely straight. When you put the ball of your foot on the pedal, you'll have the perfect slight bend.
  4. For BMX: saddle is often lower for trick riding. Only raise it for cruising.
  5. Tighten the seat clamp firmly. If using a quick-release, it should leave an imprint on your palm when closing.
Pro Tip: Mark your ideal seat height with electrical tape on the seat post so you can always return to it.

Replace Brake Pads

Medium

Worn brake pads are dangerous. Learn when and how to replace them for safe stopping.

  • Inspect current pads for wear indicators
  • Remove the wheel for easier access
  • Unbolt old pads noting orientation
  • Install new pads with toe-in alignment
  • Readjust cable tension and test

15-20 min | Tools: hex wrenches, new brake pads

Detailed Steps:
  1. Check your current pads. If the grooves are gone or you see metal, it's time to replace.
  2. Remove the wheel. This gives you better access and lets you see the pad alignment clearly.
  3. Note which way the old pad is oriented (there's usually an arrow showing rotation direction).
  4. Unbolt the old pad. On V-brakes, this is usually a single bolt with washers in a specific order — remember the order!
  5. Install the new pad. Set it so the front edge contacts the rim slightly before the rear edge (toe-in) to prevent squealing.
  6. Reinstall the wheel, squeeze the brakes, and check alignment. Adjust cable tension with the barrel adjuster.
Pro Tip: Replace both pads at the same time, even if only one is worn. Mismatched pads cause uneven braking.

E-Bike Owners

E-Bike Specific Maintenance

Electric bikes need extra care. Here's what to watch for.

Battery Care

Store at 40-80% charge. Avoid full drain or full charge for long storage. Keep between 32-100F. Charge after every ride, don't let it sit dead.

Motor & Controller

Keep motor area clean and free of debris. Check connector pins for corrosion. Listen for unusual sounds. Never pressure-wash the motor directly.

Electrical Connections

Inspect all wiring monthly. Look for frayed cables, loose connectors, or water intrusion. Apply dielectric grease to connectors in wet climates.

Brake Wear (Higher!)

E-bikes are heavier and faster, so brakes wear 2-3x faster than regular bikes. Check pads every 500 miles. Upgrade to metallic pads for better heat handling.
